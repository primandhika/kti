# Peer Review & Methodological Adaptation: 19075-article-text

## Overview
**Paper Reviewed:** Mach, T. (2025). *Assessment of Intercultural Communicative Competence in High-Stakes Speaking Tests: The case of the Czech Republic.* Journal of Language and Education, 11(3), 98-109.

This review analyzes Mach's (2025) methodology for evaluating high-stakes speaking tests, specifically to determine its transferability for a planned research study on **metacognitive speaking skills among Indonesian higher education students (L1 Bahasa Indonesia)**. Mach investigates how Czech secondary school speaking exams disproportionately assess declarative cultural knowledge rather than dynamic intercultural communication skills. This structural imbalance offers a highly replicable framework for our investigation into whether Indonesian university speaking assessments merely test "metacognitive knowledge" (declarative) rather than "metacognitive regulation" (active monitoring and planning).

---

## 1. Novelty & Transferability
**Original Paradigm:** The original study maps the tension between *Cultural Knowledge* (static facts about the US/UK) and *Intercultural Skills* (dynamic negotiation of meaning) using Larzén’s (2005) typology. 

**Indonesian Context Adaptation (High Transferability):** 
The conceptual shift required for the Indonesian context is remarkably smooth. We can directly substitute Larzén's cultural construct with a metacognitive construct (e.g., Flavell, 1979; Bachman, 1990). 
- **Declarative vs. Regulatory:** Instead of examining rubrics for "factual cultural knowledge," we will examine Indonesian higher education (PT) speaking rubrics for "declarative metacognitive tasks" (e.g., asking students to explain speech theories or structure). 
- **Skill vs. Regulation:** Instead of looking for "intercultural skills," we will code for "metacognitive regulation" (e.g., rubrics that evaluate how well a student repairs a breakdown in communication, monitors their audience, or plans their discourse in real-time).
- **Novelty:** Applying this quantitative rubric-analysis methodology to L1 Bahasa Indonesia speaking skills at the university level is highly novel. While L2/EFL speaking anxiety and strategies are widely researched, systemic assessment of L1 metacognitive speaking constructs in Indonesian PTs is a significant literature gap.

---

## 2. Empirical Rigor & Baselines
**Critique of the Original:**
Mach (2025) scraped exam topics from 206 grammar schools, representing an impressive 58% of the total target population. This sample size provides a highly robust empirical baseline. However, the textbook analysis was limited to just 4 preparatory books. While claimed as representative, 4 texts offer limited statistical power for longitudinal or comparative claims.

**Adaptation for Indonesian Higher Education:**
To match the empirical rigor of the original study's school sampling, the Indonesian PT study should:
1. **Establish a Representative Baseline:** We cannot sample 58% of all Indonesian universities (thousands exist). Instead, we must use a stratified random sample (e.g., 50 State Universities / PTN and 50 Private Universities / PTS across different islands).
2. **Material Analysis:** Instead of preparatory textbooks, the study should analyze standardized speaking course syllabi (RPS - Rencana Pembelajaran Semester) and specific grading rubrics used for midterms/finals. 
3. **Metric:** The primary baseline metric will be the percentage of assessment criteria dedicated to "cognitive/content delivery" vs. "metacognitive regulation" per RPS.

---

## 3. Reproducibility & Coding Scheme
**Critique of the Original:**
Mach’s methodology is highly reproducible. The study avoids inflating counts by using a conservative proposition-extraction method (e.g., breaking complex sentences in textbooks into distinct, single-fact propositions). Inter-rater reliability was established on 20% of the dataset.

**Adaptation for Indonesian Higher Education:**
- **Coding Framework:** We will replace Larzén's framework with a metacognitive framework. We will code RPS and rubric descriptors into distinct propositions. 
- **Verification Check:** A potential fatal issue in qualitative rubric coding is subjectivity. To prevent this, our study must clearly define operationalized verbs. For example:
  - *Declarative:* "Menjelaskan," "Menyebutkan" (Explaining, Stating).
  - *Regulatory:* "Menyesuaikan," "Memperbaiki," "Mengevaluasi" (Adjusting, Repairing, Evaluating).
- **Inter-rater Reliability:** We must replicate Mach's safeguard by having a second researcher blindly code at least 20% of the gathered Indonesian PT syllabi to establish a Cohen's Kappa score.

---

## 4. Fatal Issues & Verification
**Observation:** No fatal statistical errors were found in Mach's percentage calculations. However, a significant methodological limitation in Mach (2025) is that analyzing *topics* (or rubrics) does not perfectly reflect actual *classroom implementation or grading*. A teacher might use a "knowledge-oriented" topic but assess it using regulatory skills.
**Fix for our Study:** The Indonesian study must explicitly state this limitation. To mitigate it, our research design should ideally combine RPS/rubric document analysis with a secondary verification layer, such as a survey of higher education speaking lecturers (dosen pengampu mata kuliah Berbicara) to confirm if their actual grading practices match the documented rubrics.
