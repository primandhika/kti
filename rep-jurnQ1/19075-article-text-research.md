# Research Evidence: 19075-article-text

## Overview
- **Paper**: Assessment of Intercultural Communicative Competence in High-Stakes Speaking Tests: The case of the Czech Republic (Mach, 2025).
- **Core Premise**: High-stakes English speaking exams and their preparatory materials heavily emphasize declarative factual knowledge about culture rather than intercultural communicative competence (skills/attitudes).

## Methodology & Empirical Rigor
- **Sample 1 (Exam Topics)**: Extracted speaking exam topic lists from 206 Czech grammar schools (58% of the total population of 355 schools). Calculated the percentage of "knowledge-oriented" cultural topics (e.g., UK, USA, Literature, Prague) for each school.
- **Sample 2 (Preparatory Materials)**: Analyzed 4 popular exam preparation books spanning 2005 to 2020. 
- **Coding Framework**: Used Larzén’s (2005) typology to classify content into Factual Knowledge (realia, cultural products, traditions, modes of thought) vs. Skills vs. Affective orientation. 
- **Validation**: Independent coding by a second researcher on 20% of textbook content with differences resolved via discussion. Used a conservative counting method (e.g., breaking complex sentences into discrete factual propositions).

## Reproducibility
- The study's methodology is highly reproducible. The authors provide explicit examples of how they coded text into discrete factual propositions (e.g., separating facts about Canterbury Cathedral into distinct units of information). 
- The sampling strategy for both the schools (web scraping official lists) and the textbooks (selecting widely used, officially recommended prep books) is transparent.

## Transferability to Indonesian Context (Metacognition in L1 Speaking)
- **Conceptual Shift**: The Czech study contrasts *Cultural Knowledge* (facts about target countries) with *Intercultural Skills*. For the Indonesian context, this translates perfectly to contrasting *Metacognitive Knowledge* (knowing about speaking strategies, e.g., Flavell's person/task/strategy variables) with *Metacognitive Regulation* (the actual real-time skills of planning, monitoring, and evaluating one's speech in L1 Bahasa Indonesia).
- **Methodological Adaptation**:
  - Instead of analyzing Czech secondary school topics, the Indonesian study would analyze higher education (PT) syllabi, speaking rubrics, and exam prompts.
  - Instead of Larzén’s (2005) cultural typology, the Indonesian study would apply a metacognitive construct framework (e.g., Flavell, 1979, or Bachman's strategic competence) to code the syllabi and rubrics.
  - The quantitative approach (percentage of declarative vs. regulatory tasks in rubrics/exams) can be replicated to establish a baseline of how Indonesian universities currently assess speaking skills.
  - The sample size target should be comparable in proportion (e.g., a representative sample of state and private universities across Indonesia).
