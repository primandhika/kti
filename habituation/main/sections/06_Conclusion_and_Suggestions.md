**6. Conclusion**

This study concludes that metacognitive awareness in primary students'
speaking can be effectively habituated through consistent reflective
routines supported by digital microlearning, rather than through
explicit metacognitive instruction. Quantitative findings show a
substantial increase in observable metacognitive speaking behaviors,
with overall mean scores rising from 1.32 at baseline to 2.64 at the end
of the intervention, alongside marked growth in planning, monitoring,
and post-speaking reflection. These numerical trends are reinforced by
qualitative evidence indicating a shift from evaluative and
surface-level comments toward process-oriented reflections, as well as
by behavioral changes such as increased self-correction and voluntary
reflection, which rose from 14 percent to 71 percent across sessions.
Students' responses further indicate that digital microlearning
functioned as an effective scaffold that supported consistency and
recall of reflective routines without adding instructional burden,
confirming its role as a contextual support rather than a technological
focus. Taken together, the findings demonstrate that speaking can serve
as a viable medium for developing metacognitive regulation in primary
education when reflective practices are embedded as habits through
repetition and supported by lightweight digital tools, thereby
addressing the gap between meaningful learning ideals and routine
classroom speaking practices.
