**[1. Introduction]{.mark}**

Speaking is a foundational component of language development in primary
education, functioning not only as a means of communication but also as
a medium through which children construct meaning, organize ideas, and
demonstrate understanding. At the elementary level, speaking activities
ideally support the development of oral fluency, coherence, and
communicative confidence while simultaneously fostering higher-order
cognitive engagement. However, research across diverse educational
contexts indicates that speaking instruction in primary classrooms often
remains limited to surface-level performance, emphasizing correctness
and fluency rather than reflective language use (Goh & Burns, 2012).

In the Indonesian primary education context, speaking instruction tends
to be strongly teacher-directed and product-oriented. Classroom speaking
activities frequently take the form of answering factual questions,
retelling texts, or reproducing model sentences provided by teachers or
textbooks. While such practices may support basic oral production, they
rarely encourage learners to reflect on what they intend to say, how
they express their ideas, or how effectively their message is conveyed
to others. As a result, speaking is commonly treated as an output
activity rather than a cognitive and reflective process (Santiani &
Putra, 2021). This condition mirrors broader findings in language
education research showing that young learners' oral language
development is often constrained by instructional routines that
prioritize reproduction and memorization over meaning-making and
self-regulation (Goh & Liu, 2023).

The limited integration of metacognitive processes further compounds
this issue. Metacognitive awareness refers to learners' consciousness of
their own thinking processes and their ability to plan, monitor, and
evaluate learning activities (Flavell, 1979). In speaking, metacognitive
awareness enables learners to anticipate content before speaking,
monitor clarity and coherence during speech, and reflect on performance
afterward. These processes are particularly important for young
learners, as early exposure to metacognitive practices contributes to
the gradual development of self-regulated learning behaviours
(Whitebread et al., 2009). Despite this, metacognitive instruction in
primary education has predominantly focused on reading and writing,
while speaking remains underexplored as a domain for metacognitive
development, especially in the Indonesian context.

Existing scholarship has repeatedly shown that speaking instruction in
primary classrooms remains predominantly reproductive and procedural in
nature. Mercer (2013) and Goh and Burns (2012) note that classroom talk
is often structured to elicit brief, teacher-expected responses,
positioning students as language reproducers rather than meaning-makers.
In such contexts, learners are seldom encouraged to engage in reflective
processes before speaking, to monitor the coherence and clarity of their
utterances while speaking, or to evaluate the effectiveness of their
communication afterward (Mercer & Dawes, 2014; Zhang & Zhang, 2019).
Studies examining metacognition in oral language learning further
suggest that feedback practices in speaking lessons are largely external
and evaluative, emphasizing accuracy and fluency over reflective
self-regulation (Whitebread et al., 2009; Andrade & Brookhart, 2020).
Therefore, opportunities for learners to internalize evaluative criteria
and develop awareness of their own speaking processes remain limited.
Goh and Liu (2023) argue that when reflective support is absent,
speaking activities become disconnected from conscious cognitive
regulation, reducing students' capacity to plan, monitor, and adapt
their oral language use across contexts.

Taken together, these findings reveal a persistent mismatch between
contemporary calls for meaningful, reflective learning and the realities
of speaking pedagogy in primary education. While theoretical and policy
discourses increasingly emphasize learner awareness, self-regulation,
and deep learning (Kementerian Pendidikan Dasar dan Menengah Republik
Indonesia, 2025), everyday classroom practices continue to privilege
observable performance over the invisible metacognitive processes that
underpin effective and sustainable speaking development.

To address this limitation, the present study is grounded in the concept
of habituation as a pedagogical process. Habituation refers to the
formation of stable cognitive and behavioral patterns through repeated,
consistent practice in meaningful contexts (Lally et al., 2010). From a
developmental perspective, habituation is especially relevant for
primary school learners, as young children are more likely to
internalize cognitive routines through simple, repetitive activities
rather than abstract instruction. In this study, habituation is
conceptualized as the gradual embedding of metacognitive awareness into
students' speaking practices through recurring reflective routines.

Within this framework, metacognitive awareness in speaking is not taught
as an explicit concept but cultivated as a habitual practice embedded in
routine classroom activities. This approach aligns with calls for
pedagogical designs that support deep and meaningful learning while
remaining developmentally appropriate for primary students (Kementerian
Pendidikan Dasar dan Menengah Republik Indonesia, 2025).

Digital microlearning is positioned in this study as a supportive
learning context rather than a technological focus. Microlearning,
characterized by short, focused learning units, aligns well with the
attention span and learning preferences of primary students and has
gained popularity in elementary classrooms as a flexible digital medium
(Leong et al., 2021). When used strategically, digital microlearning can
scaffold brief reflective moments before, during, and after speaking
activities, thereby supporting the habituation of metacognitive
awareness without overburdening instructional time or teacher workload.

Despite growing interest in meaningful learning and digital integration
in primary education, a clear gap remains between these aspirations and
actual classroom practices in speaking instruction. Current approaches
rarely combine speaking, metacognitive habituation, and realistic
digital support in a coherent pedagogical design. This gap underscores
the need for empirical research that examines how metacognitive
awareness in speaking can be habituated through feasible classroom
interventions.

Accordingly, this study seeks to examine how metacognitive awareness is
habituated in primary students' speaking through digital microlearning
as a routine classroom practice. Rather than focusing on short-term
performance gains, the study adopts a process-oriented perspective by
investigating the formation of reflective speaking habits over repeated
learning cycles. Specifically, this study addresses the following
research questions: (1) how metacognitive awareness is habituated in
primary students' speaking practices through digital microlearning; (2)
what observable behavioural indicators reflect the development of
metacognitive awareness in students' speaking; and (3) how primary
students respond to the use of digital microlearning in supporting
reflective speaking routines. By answering these questions, the study
aims to bridge the gap between the demand for meaningful, reflective
learning and the prevailing reproductive practices of speaking
instruction in primary education.
