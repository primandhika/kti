**3. Methodology**

This study employed a classroom-based, process-oriented intervention
design to investigate the habituation of metacognitive awareness in
primary students' speaking practices. The methodological orientation of
the study draws on habit formation theory, which conceptualizes learning
as the gradual internalization of cognitive routines through repeated
and consistent practice in authentic contexts (Lally et al., 2010). In
educational research, such an orientation is commonly operationalized
through short-cycle classroom interventions aimed at documenting
developmental processes rather than testing causal effectiveness
(Whitebread et al., 2009; Cobb et al., 2003).

Accordingly, the study adopted a single-group descriptive pre--post
design with embedded qualitative analysis, a design frequently used in
classroom research to examine pedagogical processes that unfold over
time under natural instructional conditions (Creswell & Plano Clark,
2018). This design was selected because the focus of the study was not
to compare treatment effects across groups, but to examine how
metacognitive awareness in speaking emerged and stabilized as a habitual
practice through repeated instructional routines. Such an approach is
particularly appropriate for primary education settings, where ethical,
practical, and curricular constraints often limit the feasibility of
controlled experimental designs.

The participants were 28 Grade 5 students enrolled in a public
elementary school in Indonesia, aged between 10 and 11 years. This age
group was selected based on developmental research indicating that
children at this stage can emerge metacognitive awareness when supported
through structured and concrete learning routines (Whitebread et al.,
2009). The class was selected through convenience sampling, reflecting
an intact classroom accessible to the researcher. All students
participated as part of their regular classroom learning activities, and
no exclusion criteria were applied, as the study aimed to examine
habituation at the classroom level rather than individual differences.

The intervention was implemented over a period of four weeks, comprising
eight speaking sessions conducted twice per week. This duration was
determined with reference to habit formation research, which suggests
that observable behavioural regularities can begin to emerge through
repeated practice within a relatively short time frame, provided that
instructional routines are consistent and stable (Lally et al., 2010).
Each session followed an identical pedagogical structure to support
habituation rather than novelty-driven engagement.

Digital microlearning was used as a pedagogical scaffold to support
reflective speaking routines. The microlearning units consisted of
short, focused digital prompts accessed by students immediately before
and after speaking activities. These prompts were designed to elicit
metacognitive awareness related to planning, monitoring, and reflection,
using language appropriate for primary learners. Importantly,
metacognitive concepts were not introduced explicitly; instead, students
encountered the same reflective prompts across sessions, allowing
awareness to develop implicitly through repeated engagement. This design
choice aligns with research suggesting that young learners internalize
metacognitive routines more effectively through practice than through
abstract explanation (Veenman et al., 2006).

Each speaking session was organized into three pedagogical phases.
Before speaking, students engaged with microlearning prompts that
encouraged them to plan what they would say and how they would organize
their ideas. During speaking, students performed short oral tasks, such
as explaining a familiar topic or sharing personal experiences, while
being reminded to attend to clarity and coherence without interrupting
their speech. After speaking, students completed brief guided
reflections supported by microlearning prompts, focusing on what aspects
of their speaking were effective and what could be improved. The
reflective structure remained constant across all sessions to reinforce
habituation through repetition and consistency.

Data were collected from multiple sources to capture both observable
behavioral changes and students' reflective engagement. Observable
speaking behaviors were documented using a researcher-developed
observation rubric focused on indicators of metacognitive awareness,
including evidence of planning, monitoring, and reflection. In addition,
short written reflections produced by students were collected
periodically to capture their awareness of speaking processes. Teacher
observation notes were also gathered to document classroom dynamics and
changes in students' engagement over time. The use of multiple data
sources enabled triangulation and strengthened the credibility of the
findings (Miles et al., 2014).

Quantitative data from the observation rubric were analyzed
descriptively to identify patterns and trends in students' metacognitive
speaking behaviors across sessions. Rather than inferential testing, the
analysis focused on changes in frequency and consistency of observable
indicators, which is consistent with the process-oriented nature of
habituation research. Qualitative data from student reflections and
teacher notes were analyzed thematically using an inductive coding
approach. Initial codes were generated based on core metacognitive
dimensions, namely planning, monitoring, and reflection, and
subsequently refined to identify patterns indicative of habitual
engagement. Integration of quantitative and qualitative findings was
used to provide a coherent account of how metacognitive awareness was
habituated through the intervention (Creswell & Plano Clark, 2018).
