**5. Discussion**

This study demonstrates that primary students' speaking practices can
function as a viable medium for developing metacognitive regulation when
reflective routines are consistently habituated through digital
microlearning support. This finding extends prior metacognitive
research, which has largely focused on reading and writing, by
empirically positioning speaking as a domain for metacognitive
development in primary education (Flavell, 1979; Whitebread et al.,
2009; Goh & Liu, 2023).

In addressing the first research question, the findings suggest that
metacognitive awareness in speaking was habituated through repetition
and consistency rather than explicit instruction. The gradual emergence
of planning, monitoring, and reflection behaviors aligns with habit
formation theory, which emphasizes that stable routines embedded in
meaningful contexts support internalization of cognitive regulation
(Lally et al., 2010). For young learners, such implicit habituation is
particularly salient, as metacognitive processes are more effectively
developed through practice than through abstract explanation (Veenman et
al., 2006). The speaking activities in this study provided repeated
opportunities for learners to enact reflective behaviors, allowing
metacognitive awareness to become part of routine speaking practice
rather than an externally imposed requirement.

Regarding the second research question, the observed behavioral
indicators offer concrete evidence of metacognitive engagement in
primary students' speaking. Pausing to plan before speaking,
self-correction during speech, and reflective evaluation after speaking
correspond to the core metacognitive processes of planning, monitoring,
and evaluation identified in metacognitive theory (Flavell, 1979). The
prominence of post-speaking reflection supports previous findings that
reflection serves as an accessible entry point for metacognitive
development, particularly in oral language learning where cognitive
processes are otherwise difficult to observe (Zhang & Zhang, 2019). This
finding also corroborates research suggesting that structured reflection
enables learners to gradually assume responsibility for regulating their
own learning (Andrade & Brookhart, 2020).

The third research question concerned students' responses to digital
microlearning as a support for reflective speaking routines. The
findings indicate that microlearning functioned as an effective scaffold
by providing concise and consistent prompts that reinforced reflective
behavior without increasing instructional complexity. This supports
prior research showing that microlearning is most effective when used to
support routine formation and cognitive focus rather than as a primary
content-delivery mechanism (Leong et al., 2021). As illustrated in
**Figure 1**, digital microlearning was integrated across three phases
of speaking, before, during, and after speaking, reinforcing
metacognitive engagement as a cyclical and habitual process rather than
a one-time instructional event. The figure visually clarifies how
microlearning operated as contextual support within speaking activities,
emphasizing habituation through repetition rather than technological
novelty.

Taken together, these findings contribute to the broader discourse on
speaking pedagogy by challenging performance-oriented approaches that
prioritize fluency and accuracy over reflective engagement (Goh & Burns,
2012; Mercer & Dawes, 2014). By embedding metacognitive routines into
speaking through habituation and lightweight digital scaffolding, the
study bridges the gap between theoretical calls for meaningful,
self-regulated learning and the realities of everyday primary classroom
practice. This perspective reframes speaking not merely as oral output
but as a pedagogical site for cultivating metacognitive regulation from
an early stage of language development.
