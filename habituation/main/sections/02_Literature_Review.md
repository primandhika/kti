**2. Literature Review**

Previous research has consistently shown that speaking instruction in
primary education tends to prioritize observable performance outcomes
such as fluency, pronunciation, and accuracy, while offering limited
support for learners' reflective regulation of oral production (Goh &
Burns, 2012; Mercer, 2013). Studies on classroom talk indicate that
speaking activities are often structured to elicit brief,
teacher-expected responses, positioning students as language reproducers
rather than active meaning-makers (Mercer & Dawes, 2014; Zhang & Zhang,
2019). In contrast, metacognitive awareness, defined as learners'
ability to plan, monitor, and evaluate their own learning processes, has
been widely recognized as a key component of self-regulated learning and
sustained language development (Flavell, 1979; Veenman et al., 2006).
Empirical research in language education has demonstrated the
effectiveness of metacognitive instruction in enhancing reading and
writing outcomes across age groups (Whitebread et al., 2009; Andrade &
Brookhart, 2020). However, comparatively little attention has been given
to speaking as a domain for metacognitive development, particularly at
the primary level, where instructional practices remain largely
product-oriented (Santiani & Putra, 2021; Goh & Liu, 2023). Recent
studies suggest that metacognitive awareness in speaking is more likely
to emerge when learners engage in structured reflective routines rather
than receiving solely external feedback (Zhang & Zhang, 2019).
Additionally, microlearning has been identified as a developmentally
appropriate digital approach that supports short, focused cognitive
engagement and routine formation in young learners (Leong et al., 2021).
Nevertheless, existing literature rarely integrates speaking,
metacognitive habituation, and digital microlearning within a coherent
pedagogical design, indicating a clear gap that the present study seeks
to address.
