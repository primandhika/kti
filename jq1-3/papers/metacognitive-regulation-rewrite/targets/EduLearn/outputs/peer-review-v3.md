# Peer Review Report (EduLearn)  
**Manuscript:** `draft-v3-edulearn.md`  
**Journal context:** EduLearn (Scopus-indexed education journal)  
**Reviewer stance:** Tough but constructive

---

## 1) Originality / Novelty
### Assessment
**Moderate novelty, but currently overstated.** The manuscript’s practical contribution is the integration of three elements (microlearning input + oral explain-to-learn output + metacognitive reflection prompts) in one repeated instructional cycle for Indonesian undergraduates. That is useful and publishable. However, the paper sometimes implies mechanism-level novelty/corroboration that the design cannot prove.

### Evidence from manuscript
- Claimed contribution: *“demonstrating how generative explanation and metacognitive reflection may function as the active mechanisms…”* (Abstract; also reiterated in Introduction and Conclusion).
- Design reality: one-group pre-post classroom implementation without comparison condition (Method).

### Actionable recommendation
- Reframe novelty as **design proposition + preliminary efficacy evidence**, not mechanism demonstration.
- Replace strong mechanism language with: “consistent with,” “suggestive of,” or “plausibly explained by.”
- Add a short paragraph explicitly differentiating this study from prior microlearning-speaking studies (e.g., [13], [22], [24]) in terms of intervention architecture and measured outcomes.

---

## 2) Theoretical Framework
### Assessment
**Conceptually rich, but not yet fully integrated into a testable model.** The paper cites relevant anchors (Flavell, Zimmerman, generative learning, multimedia learning), but the framework is mostly narrative and needs explicit construct mapping.

### Evidence from manuscript
- Strong building blocks are present: metacognition [4], self-regulation [5], generative explanation [14]–[16], multimedia learning [17].
- Missing explicit model: no diagram/hypothesized pathway mapping intervention components to expected gains (e.g., why planning/evaluation should rise more than monitoring).

### Actionable recommendation
- Add a compact **conceptual model** (figure or explicit prose logic chain):
  - Micro-input (reduced extraneous load) → explanation task (generative processing) → reflection prompts (regulatory consolidation) → differential outcomes (planning/evaluation > monitoring).
- State 2–3 directional expectations before analysis (even in non-experimental studies, this improves coherence).

---

## 3) Methodology
### Assessment
**Adequate for exploratory classroom evidence, insufficient for causal inference; transparency needs strengthening.**

### Evidence from manuscript
- Design: *“classroom-based pre-post evaluation informed by a design-based research orientation”* with 80 students in intact classes.
- Positive elements: rubric dimensions are clear; two raters; ICC = .86; questionnaire reliability reported (α overall and by subscale); pilot noted.
- Key gaps:
  1. No control/comparison group; maturation and concurrent-instruction threats remain.
  2. “DBR orientation” is stated, but no iterative redesign cycles are reported (so this reads as quasi-experimental pre-post, not DBR proper).
  3. Statistical assumptions/reporting are incomplete (normality/outliers, treatment of multiplicity for several t-tests, confidence intervals).
  4. Insufficient instrumentation detail for replication (item examples, rubric descriptors/cut-points, scoring workflow/blinding).

### Actionable recommendation
- Reclassify design language to avoid DBR overreach unless iterative DBR cycles are documented.
- Add a **Method transparency block**:
  - sampling/inclusion details,
  - exact task prompts,
  - rubric descriptors (appendix/supplement),
  - whether raters were blinded to pre/post status,
  - assumption checks,
  - confidence intervals for mean differences/effect sizes,
  - multiplicity handling (or rationale for exploratory analyses).
- If possible, add sensitivity checks (e.g., nonparametric confirmation, robustness of effects).

---

## 4) Results & Discussion
### Assessment
**Generally data-aligned and thoughtfully interpreted, but some interpretive drift remains.**

### Evidence from manuscript
- Strength: Results are disaggregated (planning/monitoring/evaluation; speaking subdimensions), not just aggregate significance.
- Strength: The manuscript explicitly states associations are not mediation/causality (*“These are associations, not causal evidence…”*).
- Concern: despite caveats, some passages still read mechanistic/causal (e.g., *“active mechanisms that transform…”*).
- Concern: qualitative thematic claims are plausible but trustworthiness reporting is thin (no coding agreement indicator, audit trail summary, or exemplar balance across themes).

### Actionable recommendation
- Tighten claim discipline: keep interpretation at **association + theoretical consistency**.
- Add concise qualitative rigor details (coder agreement process evidence, negative/disconfirming cases, codebook snapshot in appendix).
- Consider a compact table linking each RQ to specific evidence and claim strength (descriptive / inferential / speculative).

---

## 5) Limitations Handling
### Assessment
**Better than average: limitations are openly acknowledged.** Remaining issue is inconsistency between limitations and some headline phrasing.

### Evidence from manuscript
- Strong limitations paragraph: single context, self-report, no control group, novelty effects, no mediation claim.
- Residual overclaim appears in Abstract and Conclusion wording that suggests demonstrated mechanisms.

### Actionable recommendation
- Harmonize all sections with the same epistemic caution level.
- Add one sentence on **external validity boundaries** (discipline/program specificity; asynchronous mode constraints).

---

## 6) Writing Quality
### Assessment
**Clear, fluent, and mostly analytical; occasionally essayistic/rhetorical.**

### Evidence from manuscript
- Strong clarity and flow overall.
- Rhetorical lines (e.g., *“But brevity is not a pedagogy.”*) improve readability but can sound editorial rather than empirical.

### Actionable recommendation
- Keep the strong narrative but trim rhetorical flourishes in Results/Conclusion.
- Increase precision markers where needed (“may,” “is consistent with,” “within this sample/context”).
- Ensure terminological consistency: “metacognitive regulation,” “self-regulation,” and “explain-to-learn” should be operationally distinguished once, then used consistently.

## 8) Remaining Weaknesses a Skeptical Reviewer 2 Would Flag
1. **No comparator condition** → cannot separate intervention effect from semester progression, instructor effects, or platform novelty.
2. **Mechanism claim overreach** relative to design.
3. **Possible common-method inflation** (self-report metacognition + same intervention context).
4. **Insufficient replication detail** for instruments/tasks and analytic decisions.
5. **Asynchronous monologic focus** limits claims about interactive speaking competence.
6. **Potential rating bias risk** unless blinding/randomization of scoring order is clarified.

### Actionable recommendation
- In this revision round, the minimum defensible path is to **tighten claims, deepen method transparency, and improve reproducibility reporting**.
- For future study: add control/comparison arms and delayed post-test.

---

## Overall Verdict
# **Major Revision**

The manuscript has a credible and relevant instructional design contribution, strong effect reporting, and better-than-usual limitation disclosure. However, for Scopus-indexed journal standards, the current version still needs major revision in claim calibration, methodological transparency, and reference-format rigor before it is publishable.

---

## Prioritized Required Changes (High → Medium)
1. **Recalibrate all causal/mechanistic language** (Abstract, Introduction, Discussion, Conclusion) to match one-group pre-post evidence.
2. **Strengthen methodological transparency** (task prompts, rubric descriptors, blinding status, assumption checks, CIs, multiplicity rationale, qualitative trustworthiness details).
3. **Clarify theoretical integration via explicit conceptual model** linking intervention components to differential outcomes.
4. **Reference list technical correction to strict IEEE consistency** (complete and standardized metadata for all 27 entries).
5. **Trim essayistic phrasing and increase precision markers** in interpretive sections.
