# EduLearn Upgrade Plan: From Draft v1 to Draft v2

## Scope
This note translates the current manuscript status into a concrete revision plan for the next EduLearn-facing version.

- **Current draft:** `papers/metacognitive-regulation-rewrite/targets/EduLearn/draft-v1-edulearn.md`
- **Next target:** `draft-v2-edulearn.md`
- **Template inspected:** `papers/metacognitive-regulation-rewrite/targets/EduLearn/template/Article-Template-edulearn.docx`
- **Guidelines inspected:** `papers/metacognitive-regulation-rewrite/targets/EduLearn/guidelines/AUTHOR_GUIDELINES_EDULEARN.md`

## What the EduLearn template implies
The template and guidelines together suggest that EduLearn expects not only correct section labels, but also a particular rhetorical shape.

### 1. Title should be short and information-dense
Observed from the template and guidelines:
- title should be succinct
- avoid waste phrases such as *A study of*, *The effect of*, *Analysis of*, *Design of*
- guidelines say **max 10 words**
- template text also says roughly **no more than about 12 words**
- avoid acronyms and abbreviations in the title

**Implication for our paper:**
Current title is strong conceptually, but still needs one final check against the stricter 10-word guideline.

Current title:
- **Explain-to-Learn Microlearning for Metacognitive Regulation in Academic Speaking**

Word count depends on hyphen treatment, but it is likely still acceptable only if EduLearn counts hyphenated forms compactly. We should keep it provisionally, but prepare one shorter backup title.

Possible backup options:
- **Explain-to-Learn Microlearning in Academic Speaking**
- **Microlearning for Metacognitive Academic Speaking**
- **Explain-to-Learn for Academic Speaking Regulation**

### 2. Abstract must be compact, self-contained, and quantitative
Observed from the template:
- abstract should stand alone
- it should state problem, approach, findings, and conclusion
- target length: **100–200 words**
- no literature citations in abstract
- 5–7 keywords

**Implication for our paper:**
The revised abstract is directionally correct because it now includes numeric findings, but v2 should check:
- final word count under 200
- no unnecessary framing language
- only the strongest quantitative results remain
- conclusion line is concise and journal-like

### 3. Introduction should be compact and function-driven
Observed from the template:
- introduction should ideally work in **3–6 paragraphs**
- it should clearly contain:
  1. background
  2. problem
  3. relevant literature
  4. proposed solution
  5. novelty/new value
- at least 10 references in the introduction
- IEEE citation style

**Implication for our paper:**
The current introduction is much sharper than before, but v2 should still compress it into a tighter EduLearn rhythm. It currently behaves more like a strong journal draft than a template-matched EduLearn introduction.

What still needs tightening:
- reduce repetition between academic speaking demands and metacognitive regulation claims
- make the **problem sentence** even more visible
- make the **novelty claim** unmistakable in the final paragraph
- shift all citations to IEEE numbering

### 4. Method should sound reproducible and linear
Observed from the template:
- method should explain design, procedures, testing, and data acquisition clearly
- literature can support the method section
- figures/tables may appear if they clarify procedures

**Implication for our paper:**
Our method already has a coherent flow, but v2 should make it feel more reproducible and less essay-like.

Needed upgrades:
- specify the intervention sequence more crisply
- state instruments with enough precision for reuse
- report inter-rater agreement and reliability more concretely if those values are available in the source materials
- clarify the analysis order: descriptive -> inferential -> thematic
- decide whether the study is best framed as:
  - **design-based research orientation with pre-post evaluation**, or
  - **pre-post intervention study informed by design-based logic**

This framing must be consistent across abstract, method, results, and conclusion.

### 5. Results and Discussion can use sub-sections
Observed from the template:
- results and discussion are combined
- sub-sections are allowed
- statistical results should be central
- figures/tables should be cited in text
- results should not be redundantly repeated across prose and tables

**Implication for our paper:**
The current Results and Discussion section is structurally compatible with EduLearn, but still needs v2 refinement.

Needed upgrades:
- front-load the strongest results earlier
- trim interpretive repetition after each table
- make every table explicitly referenced in nearby prose
- ensure discussion compares findings to prior studies more directly
- keep caution about causal language

### 6. Conclusion must mirror the introduction
Observed from the template:
- conclusion should show compatibility between introduction and results
- it may mention future development and further application

**Implication for our paper:**
The conclusion is already cautious and coherent, but v2 should make the final paragraph more explicitly answer the original problem:
- why this matters for academic speaking instruction
- what exactly was added beyond existing microlearning use
- what the study can and cannot claim

### 7. Back matter is expected and partly mandatory
Observed from the template:
- acknowledgments optional
- funding information mandatory
- author contributions statement mandatory
- corresponding author details expected

**Implication for our paper:**
We already include several end sections, but v2 should check whether EduLearn expects:
- corresponding author block in template format
- CRediT-style author contribution wording aligned more closely with template language
- whether conflict of interest, ethics, consent, and data availability should remain in current prose or be adapted to house style

## Gap analysis: current v1 vs EduLearn v2 target

### Already improved in v1
- title aligned better with the paper’s actual contribution
- abstract now includes quantitative findings
- introduction now states a clearer instructional gap
- citation prose is less monotonous than before
- results are statistically explicit
- conclusion is reasonably cautious

### Still missing or incomplete for v2
1. **IEEE citation conversion** is still not done.
2. **Reference count is still too low** for EduLearn expectations.
   - Current draft has about 18 references.
   - EduLearn usually expects **25–30**, primarily journal articles.
3. **Introduction still needs compression** into a more template-like cadence.
4. **Method still needs harder reproducibility detail**.
5. **Journal formatting logic** is not yet reflected in the draft structure.
6. **Title length risk** remains unresolved.
7. **Back matter alignment** with the template is not yet checked carefully.

## Concrete v2 revision tasks

## Task 1 — Convert all citations and references to IEEE style
**Why:** This is the single clearest journal-compliance gap.

Actions:
- replace all author-year in-text citations with numbered IEEE citations
- preserve a few narrative mentions where useful, e.g. `Paterson [12] argued ...`
- reorder the reference list by first appearance in text
- ensure ranges and grouped citations follow EduLearn examples: `[3]`, `[5]-[7]`, `[2], [9], [11]`

Output target:
- fully converted reference system in `draft-v2-edulearn.md`

## Task 2 — Expand the reference base to EduLearn-safe depth
**Why:** Current reference count is likely below journal expectations.

Actions:
- add targeted recent journal references in these areas:
  - academic speaking pedagogy
  - metacognitive regulation in L2 speaking
  - microlearning in language learning
  - self-explanation / explain-to-learn / generative learning
  - technology-enhanced speaking instruction
- prioritize journal articles over books and websites
- aim for **25–30 references** total
- ensure **20–30% are recent papers**

Output target:
- reference list expanded without padding or irrelevant citations

## Task 3 — Tighten the Introduction into a cleaner EduLearn shape
**Why:** EduLearn favors explicit rhetorical function over broad theoretical wandering.

Actions:
- keep the current argument, but compress it into roughly **5 paragraphs**:
  1. importance of academic speaking
  2. problem: product-oriented instruction under-supports metacognitive regulation
  3. limitation of microlearning alone
  4. explain-to-learn as proposed instructional solution
  5. contribution, novelty, and research questions
- make one sentence unmistakably state:
  - **what is missing in prior work**, and
  - **what this study contributes that is new**

Output target:
- shorter, sharper introduction with stronger problem visibility

## Task 4 — Make the Method more reproducible
**Why:** The current method reads well, but EduLearn-style review may ask for more procedural clarity.

Actions:
- keep one main Method section, but strengthen internal sequence:
  - design and context
  - participants
  - intervention flow
  - instruments
  - analysis
- if available from the source materials, add:
  - rubric reliability detail
  - questionnaire reliability coefficients
  - expert validation detail
  - timeline or module count clarity
- define the analysis workflow more explicitly

Output target:
- method that is concise but easier to replicate

## Task 5 — Trim repetition in Results and Discussion
**Why:** Some passages still restate what the tables already show.

Actions:
- keep the strongest statistics in prose
- reduce table-reading narration
- move from result description to interpretation faster
- preserve the key caution that correlations do not prove mediation
- strengthen direct comparison with prior studies in each major subsection

Output target:
- more efficient results/discussion with higher journal readability

## Task 6 — Strengthen the conclusion-to-introduction match
**Why:** The template explicitly wants compatibility between opening problem and final claims.

Actions:
- restate the original problem in compressed form
- state the contribution in one sharp sentence
- keep causal caution
- end with one practical forward-looking sentence for academic speaking instruction

Output target:
- conclusion that closes the exact argument opened in the introduction

## Task 7 — Align the manuscript with template metadata and back matter
**Why:** Even strong manuscripts get bounced for avoidable style mismatch.

Actions:
- verify keyword count is 5–7
- prepare corresponding-author block in template style
- check whether acknowledgments wording is necessary and minimal
- align funding statement with EduLearn’s template expectation
- review author contributions so they fit CRediT language cleanly
- check whether ethics/consent/data-availability statements should be retained as-is or compressed for journal fit

Output target:
- back matter that looks submission-ready, not merely drafted

## Recommended v2 order of work
1. Freeze the title choice.
2. Convert citations to IEEE style.
3. Expand and reorder the references.
4. Tighten the introduction.
5. Upgrade the method for reproducibility.
6. Trim and sharpen results/discussion.
7. Refit the conclusion.
8. Align back matter with template conventions.
9. Export to Word using the EduLearn template.

## Proposed quality bar for v2
Draft v2 should satisfy all of the following:
- title acceptable for EduLearn length/style constraints
- abstract under 200 words and numerically informative
- introduction clearly shows background, problem, solution, novelty
- all citations in IEEE style
- references expanded to a safer journal-level range
- method concise but reproducible
- results/discussion statistically explicit and non-redundant
- conclusion tightly matched to the initial problem and contribution
- back matter aligned with EduLearn expectations

## Practical note
The current v1 is already a meaningful substantive revision. The next move to v2 should focus less on broad rewriting and more on **journal conversion with selective strengthening**. The largest remaining gaps are not the core argument anymore; they are **citation system, reference depth, template compliance, and tighter rhetorical compression**.
