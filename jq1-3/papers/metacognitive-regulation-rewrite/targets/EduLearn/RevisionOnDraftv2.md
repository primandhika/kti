# Reviewer Revision Notes (Direct Revision-Oriented Feedback)

## 1. Overclaiming Causality

### Problematic sentence

> “The intervention likely worked not by delivering content more efficiently, but by forcing a repeated cycle of reorganization, expression, and reflection...”

### Why problematic

The study uses a one-group pre-post design without a control group. Therefore, causal interpretation is methodologically weak.

The phrase:

> “the intervention worked”

sounds experimentally conclusive despite the absence of comparison conditions.

### Suggested revision

Replace with:

> “The observed gains may be associated with the repeated cycle of reorganization, expression, and reflection embedded within the instructional design.”

Or:

> “The findings suggest that the intervention may have supported students’ speaking development through repeated opportunities for explanation and reflective regulation.”

---

## 2. Insufficient Instrument Validation

### Problematic sentence

> “All instruments were reviewed by subject-matter experts and piloted before use.”

### Why problematic

This statement is too vague for an international journal article.

No evidence is provided regarding:

* reliability,
* internal consistency,
* rubric calibration,
* construct validity,
* pilot sample characteristics.

### Suggested revision

Expand with details such as:

> “The metacognitive regulation questionnaire demonstrated acceptable internal consistency (Cronbach’s α = .87 overall; planning α = .81; monitoring α = .79; evaluation α = .84). The speaking rubric was adapted from previous academic speaking assessment frameworks and calibrated through two rater training sessions. Inter-rater reliability reached ICC = .86.”

Without reliability evidence, reviewers may question whether the observed gains reflect actual learning improvement or measurement instability.

---

## 3. Statistical Inconsistency

### Problematic sentence

> “Speaking performance showed a large overall gain from pre-test to post-test (t(79) = 9.87...)”

### Problematic table

> “DIMENSION T(95)”

### Why problematic

The manuscript reports 80 participants, yet the table reports:

> t(95)

This inconsistency seriously damages statistical credibility.

### Suggested revision

The authors must:

* verify sample size,
* verify degrees of freedom,
* ensure consistency between text and tables.

If paired-sample t-tests were used with N = 80:

> t(79)

is likely correct.

This issue MUST be corrected before submission.

---

## 4. Theoretical Contribution Still Underdeveloped

### Problematic sentence

> “The practical implication is narrower than a general endorsement of microlearning for speaking.”

### Why problematic

The paper still frames itself primarily as a “microlearning effectiveness” study, which risks appearing incremental.

Current positioning:

* microlearning,
* speaking,
* metacognition,
  has already been widely discussed in prior literature.

### Suggested revision

The paper should foreground the PROCESS mechanism more strongly.

Suggested reframing:

> “The contribution of this study lies not merely in applying microlearning to academic speaking, but in demonstrating how generative explanation and metacognitive reflection may function as the active mechanisms that transform brief instructional exposure into deeper speaking regulation.”

This repositioning significantly strengthens theoretical novelty.

---

## 5. Qualitative Findings Are Too Thin

### Problematic sentence

> “Three themes recurred across the dataset...”

### Why problematic

The manuscript claims thematic analysis but does not provide:

* coding procedures,
* participant excerpts,
* theme construction process,
* analytical depth.

Currently, the qualitative section reads more like a summary than rigorous qualitative analysis.

### Suggested revision

Add:

* representative quotations,
* coding explanation,
* theme derivation process.

Example:

> “One participant stated, ‘When I explained the material aloud, I realized I actually did not fully understand the concept during the video session.’ This illustrates how explanation tasks functioned as metacognitive triggers rather than mere speaking practice.”

Including participant voices will substantially improve credibility.

---

## 6. Methodological Details Are Too Limited

### Problematic sentence

> “The intervention ran for eight weeks within a web-based microlearning environment...”

### Why problematic

The instructional implementation remains too abstract.

Reviewers will likely ask:

* What platform was used?
* How long was each session?
* How many modules per week?
* What was the average speaking duration?
* Were prompts synchronous or asynchronous?

### Suggested revision

Add operational details such as:

* LMS/platform name,
* module duration,
* speaking task duration,
* reflection structure,
* weekly schedule,
* instructor involvement.

Example:

> “Each microlearning session lasted approximately 20–25 minutes and consisted of a 5-minute multimedia input, a 7-minute recorded oral explanation task, and a 5-minute metacognitive reflection sequence delivered asynchronously through Google Classroom and Flip.”

---

## 7. Discussion Section Needs More Criticality

### Problematic sentence

> “The findings suggest that microlearning becomes more instructionally useful...”

### Why problematic

The discussion mainly reinforces supportive literature and lacks engagement with contradictory perspectives.

The paper currently sounds too confirmatory.

### Suggested revision

Add discussion regarding:

* risks of fragmented learning in microlearning environments,
* superficial reflection behavior,
* limitations of asynchronous monologic speaking,
* potential novelty effects from digital platforms.

Example:

> “Although microlearning may reduce cognitive overload, excessively segmented instruction may also risk conceptual fragmentation if generative integration tasks are absent.”

This would make the discussion more analytically mature.

---

## 8. Ethics Statement Is Weak

### Problematic sentence

> “No separate ethics approval number was reported...”

### Why problematic

Many Scopus-indexed journals require formal ethics documentation.

This sentence may trigger immediate reviewer concern.

### Suggested revision

Strengthen institutional justification.

Example:

> “The study followed institutional ethical guidelines for classroom-based educational research involving voluntary participation, informed consent, anonymity, and non-evaluative participation. According to institutional policy at the time of data collection, formal ethics board approval was not required for minimal-risk pedagogical studies.”

If possible, obtain retrospective institutional clarification.

---

## 9. Template Artifacts Reduce Professionalism

### Problematic examples

> “Month 2099”
> “Paper’s should be the fewest possible...”

### Why problematic

These leftover template artifacts create the impression of careless manuscript preparation.

Even strong studies can receive negative reviewer impressions from such formatting issues.

### Suggested revision

Carefully clean:

* placeholders,
* issue numbering,
* headers,
* template instructions,
* inconsistent typography.

The manuscript should appear publication-ready before submission.

---

## 10. Discussion Sometimes Sounds Essayistic Rather Than Scientific

### Problematic sentence

> “The gains in this study were real...”

### Why problematic

The tone occasionally becomes rhetorical rather than analytical.

High-impact journals generally prefer:

* analytical precision,
* cautious interpretation,
* evidence-centered phrasing.

### Suggested revision

Replace with:

> “Substantial pre-post gains were observed across multiple dimensions of metacognitive regulation and academic speaking performance.”

This preserves meaning while sounding more scientific.

---

# Final Reviewer Position

The manuscript demonstrates:

* a coherent conceptual structure,
* relevant educational significance,
* promising instructional integration,
* and statistically meaningful findings.

However, the current version still resembles a strong exploratory or pilot study rather than a fully mature international journal article.

The paper’s publishability would increase substantially if the authors:

1. strengthen methodological transparency,
2. reduce causal overstatement,
3. deepen theoretical contribution,
4. provide stronger validation evidence,
5. and enrich qualitative analysis.

At present, the manuscript is potentially suitable for publication after MAJOR REVISION.
