Abstract
This        design-based study investigates how technology-mediated explain-to-learn
 activities foster metacognitive regulation as a mediating mechanism for academic
  speaking development. Grounded in the Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning
 (CTML) and self-regulated learning theory, the study examined the integration of
    microlearning and the Feynman technique among 96 Indonesian undergraduate
students over eight weeks. Results     revealed significant improvements          in
metacognitive regulation (Wilks' Λ = .41, p < .001, partial η² = .59) and academic
 speaking performance (Cohen's d = 1.01), with the strongest effects observed in
planning and evaluation dimensions. Analysis of student perceptions indicated high
   perceived usefulness and cognitive engagement. The findings demonstrate that
metacognitive regulation functions as a critical pathway linking explain-to-learn
instructional design to speaking performance,   offering evidence-based       design
 principles for technology-enhanced language education. The study contributes to
understanding how    generative learning strategies embedded in        microlearning
environments can systematically activate regulatory processes essential for academic
communication competence.

       Keywords: metacognitive regulation; explain-to-learn; microlearning; Feynman
       technique; academic speaking; design-based research; generative learning

Introduction
The    increasing  integration   of digital    technologies into higher   education    has
fundamentally reshaped how academic skills are taught and learned, particularly in
language education. Over the past decade, educational technology research has shifted
from examining access and delivery toward understanding how instructional design can
support higher-order cognitive and metacognitive processes (Mayer, 2014; Bond et al.,
2020). Within this landscape, academic speaking has emerged as a critical competence
for university students, functioning not only as a communicative skill but also as a
mechanism for knowledge construction, argumentation, and disciplinary participation
(Zhu & Carless, 2018; Goh & Liu, 2023). Despite its importance, academic speaking
instruction in higher education remains largely performance-oriented, often emphasizing
fluency and linguistic accuracy while paying limited attention to the metacognitive
processes that underpin effective spoken communication (Baines et al., 2025).

From a cognitive and educational psychology perspective, successful academic speaking
requires  learners to  actively  regulate their thinking before, during,    and after oral
performance.   Metacognitive     regulation,   encompassing planning,   monitoring,    and
evaluation, has been identified as a key factor influencing learners' ability to organize
ideas,         adapt explanations to audiences, and reflect on communicative effectiveness
(Flavell, 1979;       Magno, 2010;  Zimmerman,  2002).   Empirical studies     in language
education consistently demonstrate that learners with higher levels of metacognitive
regulation exhibit stronger speaking performance and greater capacity for self-directed
improvement (Goh & Liu, 2023; Sabnani & Goh, 2022). However, in many higher
education contexts, metacognitive instruction remains implicit or incidental rather than
systematically embedded within speaking pedagogy (Zhang et al., 2021).

Concurrently, the rise of digital-native learners has intensified calls for instructional
designs   that  align  with learners' cognitive characteristics   and learning     habits.
Microlearning has gained prominence as a pedagogical approach that delivers content in
short, focused       segments     designed to reduce cognitive overload and support active

processing  (Hug & Friesen,        2007; Susilana et al., 2022). Research in educational
technology suggests that microlearning is particularly effective when combined with
reflective and generative learning activities, rather than being used solely for content
transmission (Noetel et al., 2022). Grounded in the Cognitive Theory of Multimedia
Learning, segmented and multimodal instruction can facilitate deeper processing when
learners  are required      to actively organize and integrate information (Mayer, 2014;
Fiorella & Mayer, 2016). Nevertheless, studies also caution that microlearning, if poorly
designed, risks fragmenting understanding and reinforcing surface learning (De Jong,
2010; Jahnke et al., 2020).

One instructional strategy with strong potential to complement microlearning is the
Feynman technique, an explain-to-learn approach that requires learners to articulate
concepts in their own words and identify gaps in understanding through explanation
(Reyes et al., 2021; Paterson, 2022). Rooted in principles of generative learning and
metacognitive awareness, the Feynman technique has been shown to promote deeper
conceptual    understanding, reflective thinking, and    communicative clarity    across
disciplines (Goldhawk, 2024; Fiorella & Mayer, 2016). In language education, explaining
concepts aloud aligns closely with the cognitive demands of academic speaking, as
learners must plan explanations, monitor clarity, and evaluate audience comprehension in
real time (Goh & Liu, 2023). Despite these theoretical synergies, empirical research
integrating microlearning and the Feynman technique to support metacognitive regulation
in academic speaking remains limited, particularly in higher education contexts.

Within the Indonesian higher education landscape, recent policy orientations further
underscore the relevance of this issue. The Kampus Berdampak paradigm emphasizes
outcome-   and impact-based  learning, positioning communication   competence   as     a
strategic graduate attribute for knowledge  dissemination     and  societal   engagement
(Yuliarto et al., 2025). Similarly, the framework of Pembelajaran Mendalam highlights
communication and metacognitive reflection as core dimensions of graduate profiles,
calling for instructional practices that move beyond rote learning toward meaningful
knowledge     construction  (Kementerian Pendidikan Dasar     dan  Menengah     Republik

Indonesia, 2025). These policy directions amplify the need for instructional designs that
explicitly integrate technology, metacognition, and academic communication.

Against this backdrop, the present study adopts a design-based research approach to
examine the integration of microlearning and the Feynman technique as a means of
fostering metacognitive regulation in academic speaking within higher education. By
iteratively designing, implementing, and evaluating a technology-mediated instructional
intervention, this study seeks to contribute empirical evidence on how explain-to-learn
principles  embedded  in microlearning environments can    support deeper    learning
processes in academic speaking. Specifically, this study addresses the following research
questions 1) to what extent does the integration of microlearning and the Feynman
technique   influence students' metacognitive regulation   (planning, monitoring, and
evaluation) in academic speaking tasks? 2) How does instructional intervention affect
students' academic speaking performance across different dimensions (idea organization,
clarity of explanation, strategic delivery, and responsiveness)? 3) What are students'
perceptions regarding the usefulness, cognitive engagement, and instructional clarity of
the integrated microlearning and explain-to-learn approach?


Literature Review

Academic Speaking and Metacognitive Regulation in Higher Education
In higher education, academic speaking is no longer conceived merely as an oral
extension of linguistic knowledge but as a complex cognitive activity through which
learners externalize, negotiate, and reconstruct disciplinary understanding (Zhu          &
Carless, 2018; Mercer, 2018). Speaking in academic contexts requires students to do
more than "talk fluently"; they must plan ideas, structure arguments, anticipate audiences,
and evaluate the effectiveness of their explanations in real time (Goh & Liu, 2023). In
this sense, academic  speaking can   be likened  to thinking  aloud   under       cognitive
pressure—verbalizing  thought while  simultaneously monitoring its  coherence           and
relevance.

From the  perspective of educational  psychology,   this process is        inseparable from
metacognitive regulation. Metacognitive regulation refers to learners' ability to plan,

monitor, and evaluate their cognitive activities while performing a task (Flavell, 1979;
Magno, 2010; Schraw, 1998). In speaking tasks, these processes manifest concretely: for
example, when a student outlines key points before a presentation (planning), adjusts
explanations in response to audience cues (monitoring), or reflects on the clarity of
arguments  after   speaking  (evaluation).  Empirical  studies consistently indicate    that
learners with stronger     metacognitive    regulation demonstrate higher-quality       oral
performance and greater autonomy in improving their speaking skills (Sabnani & Goh,
2022; Zhang et al., 2021; Teng & Zhang, 2016).

Despite  this evidence, academic speaking   instruction        in universities often remains
product-focused,    emphasizing  final     performance  outcomes     such   as      fluency,
pronunciation, or lexical accuracy while marginalizing the cognitive and metacognitive
processes that generate those outcomes (Baines et al., 2025; Goh, 2018). This situation
mirrors  what has   been     described in  other educational   domains as   "teaching    the
performance without teaching the thinking behind it" (Magno, 2010, p. 42). As a result,
students may appear competent in rehearsed situations but struggle to transfer speaking
skills to new academic contexts.

Microlearning as Instructional Design for Deep Processing
In response to the learning preferences of digital-native students, microlearning has
gained prominence as an instructional design approach that delivers content in short,
focused, and modular units (Hug & Friesen, 2007; Susilana et al., 2022). Rather than
functioning as a simplified version of traditional instruction, microlearning is intended to
reduce   cognitive  overload    and support active  processing by  segmenting        complex
information into manageable learning episodes (Mayer, 2014; Fiorella & Mayer, 2016).
One may think of microlearning as intellectual scaffolding in small steps, where each step
is designed to be cognitively digestible but conceptually connected to a larger structure.

Research in educational technology suggests that microlearning can enhance engagement
and retention, particularly when learning objectives are clearly defined and aligned with
reflective activities (Susilana et al., 2022; Noetel et al., 2022). However, scholars have
also cautioned against viewing microlearning as a panacea. When used merely to transmit
fragmented    information,   it risks    promoting   surface  learning  and       conceptual

disconnection, much like assembling puzzle pieces without a picture of the whole (De
Jong, 2010; Jahnke et al., 2020). Meta-analytic evidence further indicates that the
effectiveness of multimedia and microlearning designs depends heavily on how learners
are prompted to actively    organize and integrate information, rather than passively
consume content (Noetel et al., 2022; Fiorella & Mayer, 2016).

Within the context of language education, microlearning has been applied primarily to
vocabulary acquisition, grammar practice, and receptive skills (Susilana et al., 2022;
Kasneci et al., 2023). Its application to productive skills such as academic speaking
remains comparatively    underexplored, particularly   with   respect to    fostering
metacognitive regulation rather than procedural repetition (Goh & Liu, 2023). This gap
suggests that microlearning requires complementary pedagogical strategies that explicitly
engage learners in reflective and generative processes.

The Feynman Technique as an Explain-to-Learn Strategy
One instructional strategy that aligns strongly with metacognitive and generative learning
principles is the Feynman technique. Rooted in the idea that "what cannot be explained
simply is not yet understood," the Feynman technique requires learners to explain
concepts in their own words, identify gaps in understanding, and iteratively refine
explanations (Reyes et al., 2021; Paterson, 2022). In educational terms, it operationalizes
the principle of explain-to-learn, whereby explanation is not a by-product of learning but
its driving mechanism (Paterson, 2022; Chi & Wylie, 2014).

The explanatory process inherent in the Feynman technique closely mirrors the cognitive
demands of academic speaking. Explaining a concept orally requires learners to select
essential information, organize it coherently, and adapt it to an imagined or real audience,
all while monitoring clarity and accuracy (Goh & Liu, 2023). In this sense, the Feynman
technique can be seen as a metacognitive mirror, forcing learners to confront what they
truly understand versus what they merely recognize. Empirical studies across disciplines
have shown that explain-to-learn strategies enhance conceptual understanding, critical
thinking, and self-regulated learning (Goldhawk, 2024; Reyes et al., 2021; Fiorella &
Mayer, 2016).

Nevertheless, research integrating the Feynman technique into technology-mediated
environments remains limited, particularly in higher education language instruction. Most
existing studies examine explanation as a classroom-based or peer-interaction activity,
leaving open questions about how digital instructional designs can systematically embed
explain-to-learn cycles and support metacognitive regulation over time.


Theoretical Framework
This study is grounded in two complementary theoretical perspectives: the Cognitive
Theory of Multimedia Learning (CTML) and self-regulated learning (SRL) theory. These
frameworks collectively inform   the  instructional design and provide a lens      for
interpreting how the integration of microlearning and the Feynman technique fosters
metacognitive regulation in academic speaking.

Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning (CTML)
CTML posits that meaningful learning occurs when learners engage in active cognitive
processing—selecting relevant    information,     organizing it into   coherent mental
representations, and integrating it with prior knowledge (Mayer, 2014). The theory
emphasizes that instructional design should manage cognitive load through segmentation
and signaling while promoting generative processing through active learning strategies.

As illustrated in Table 1, the Feynman technique operationalizes all three cognitive
processes of  CTML:    learners  must select     essential information when  preparing

explanations (selecting), organize ideas    coherently  when   articulating       concepts
(organizing), and connect new understanding with prior knowledge when identifying and
addressing  gaps (integrating). This alignment between  instructional strategy         and
cognitive processing theory provides the theoretical foundation for hypothesizing that
explain-to-learn activities embedded in microlearning environments will foster deeper
learning outcomes.

Self-Regulated Learning (SRL) Theory
SRL theory complements CTML by foregrounding the metacognitive processes that
govern            learning (Zimmerman, 2002; Schraw, 1998). According to this perspective,
effective learners actively plan their approach to tasks, monitor their understanding and
performance during execution, and evaluate outcomes to inform future learning. These
regulatory processes are particularly critical for complex skills like academic speaking,
where real-time cognitive demands require ongoing self-management.

The integration of microlearning and the Feynman technique in this study was designed
to systematically scaffold these metacognitive processes through structured pre-task,
during-task, and post-task activities. Planning is supported through pre-speaking prompts
that encourage learners to outline key points and anticipate audience needs. Monitoring is
activated during the explanation phase as learners must attend to clarity and coherence in
real time. Evaluation is facilitated through post-speaking reflection questions that prompt
learners to assess their performance and identify areas for improvement. This tripartite
structure aligns with the cyclical nature of self-regulated learning and provides multiple
opportunities for metacognitive engagement throughout the learning process.

Integration of CTML and SRL in Research Design
The theoretical integration of CTML and SRL provides a comprehensive framework for
understanding how the instructional intervention operates. CTML explains the cognitive
mechanisms   through which explain-to-learn activities promote  learning       (generative
processing), while SRL theory illuminates the regulatory mechanisms that govern how
learners manage their own learning processes. Together, these theories predict that the
integration of microlearning (structural support for cognitive  processing) and        the
Feynman technique (generative strategy activating regulatory processes) will produce

synergistic effects on both metacognitive regulation and academic speaking performance.

Research Method

Participants
The study involved 96 undergraduate students (Age M = 20.4 years, SD = 1.2; Females n
= 62, 64.6%; Males n = 34, 35.4%) enrolled in a Language Education program at a public
university in Indonesia. All participants were native speakers of Indonesian and had
completed at least one year of formal academic speaking coursework prior to the study.
The cohort can therefore be described as linguistically homogeneous in terms of first
language  background,   yet heterogeneous in  academic speaking proficiency                    and
metacognitive awareness, a condition deemed suitable for design-based research focusing
on instructional processes rather than population generalization (McKenney & Reeves,
2019).

Participants were recruited through intact classes to preserve ecological validity, as the
intervention was embedded   within regular instructional     activities (i.e.,           authentic
classroom conditions rather than laboratory manipulation). Participation was voluntary,
and informed consent was obtained prior to data collection. Ethical considerations
followed  institutional research guidelines and principles   of educational               research
involving               adult learners, including anonymity, non-evaluative participation, and the
absence of grading incentives (Creswell & Plano Clark, 2018).

Research Design
This study   adopted                     a design-based research (DBR) approach to examine how the
integration of microlearning and the Feynman technique fosters metacognitive regulation

in   academic speaking. DBR     was selected because it allows     for the    systematic
investigation of learning processes while simultaneously refining instructional design
through iterative cycles of   implementation and analysis      (Design-Based    Research
Collective, 2003; McKenney & Reeves, 2019; Wang & Hannafin, 2005).

The research design unfolded across three iterative phases over eight weeks:

1.  Analysis  and exploration (Week 1): Focusing on learners' needs, difficulties in
academic speaking, and baseline assessment of metacognitive regulation.

2. Design and construction (Weeks 2-3): Involving the development of a web-based
microlearning environment embedding explain-to-learn         (Feynman-based) activities,
followed by pilot testing.

3. Implementation and evaluation (Weeks 4-8): Where the instructional design was
enacted across multiple cycles, with ongoing refinement based on formative feedback,
culminating in post-intervention assessment.

Rather than testing a single causal hypothesis in isolation, the study sought to identify
design principles and learning mechanisms (i.e., how and why the intervention works), a
defining characteristic of DBR (Reeves, 2006; McKenney & Reeves, 2019).

Instructional Intervention
The intervention consisted of a web-based microlearning platform designed to support
academic speaking through segmented learning units (bite-sized modules). The platform
was developed using Moodle with custom plugins to support audio recording and
self-reflection features. Each module focused on a specific speaking function (e.g.,
explaining concepts, structuring arguments, responding to questions) and followed a
consistent instructional sequence:

1.   Concept exposure (5-7 minutes): Students engaged with short multimedia input
including video explanations, infographics, and annotated examples.

2. Explain-to-learn task (10-15 minutes): Students explained the concept orally using
their own words, recording their explanation via the platform (Feynman technique).

3. Metacognitive reflection (5  minutes):          Students responded to structured prompts
encouraging planning (before speaking), monitoring (during explanation), and evaluation
(after performance).

This structure was  grounded    in the  Cognitive Theory     of  Multimedia       Learning,
particularly the principles of        segmentation, limited capacity, and active processing
(Mayer, 2014). The Feynman technique functioned as a generative strategy, transforming
microlearning from a content-delivery mechanism into a reflective learning process
(Reyes et al., 2021; Fiorella & Mayer, 2016).

Instruments and Data Collection
Data were collected using three complementary instruments to capture both outcomes
and processes:

Academic Speaking Performance Test
Speaking performance was assessed through task-based oral presentations aligned with
course   objectives. The    assessment  rubric measured    four  dimensions: (a)       idea
organization, (b) clarity of explanation, (c) strategic delivery, and (d) responsiveness to
audience cues. Each dimension was rated on a 5-point scale (1 = poor, 5 = excellent) by
two independent raters. The rubric was adapted from established frameworks in academic
speaking assessment (Zhu & Carless, 2018; Goh & Liu, 2023). Inter-rater reliability was
satisfactory (Cohen's κ = .84 for pre-test, .87 for post-test).

Metacognitive Regulation Questionnaire
Metacognitive regulation was measured using a self-report questionnaire focusing on
three dimensions: planning (5 items), monitoring (5 items), and evaluation (5 items).
Items were adapted from validated metacognitive instruments and contextualized for
academic speaking tasks (Magno, 2010; Sabnani & Goh, 2022). Example items include:
"Before speaking, I plan how to organize my ideas" (planning), "While explaining, I
check whether my audience understands" (monitoring), and "After speaking, I reflect on
what I could improve" (evaluation). Responses were recorded on a 5-point Likert scale (1
= strongly disagree, 5 = strongly agree) to capture sufficient variance while maintaining
cognitive accessibility for respondents.

Learner Perception Survey
To capture         learners' experiences with the instructional design, a perception survey
examined   perceived  usefulness, cognitive engagement,  and clarity of       instructional
structure using 8 items (e.g., "The short learning units helped me focus on explaining
ideas more     clearly," "Explaining  concepts in my     own words     improved          my
understanding").    This instrument   provided qualitative support  for   interpreting
quantitative trends (Creswell & Plano Clark, 2018).

Validity and Reliability
Content validity of the instruments was ensured through expert review by two scholars in
language education and educational technology. Pilot testing was conducted with a small
group of students (n = 15) sharing similar characteristics to the main participants,
allowing for item refinement and clarity adjustments.

Internal consistency  reliability was         examined using Cronbach's alpha. All retained
subscales exceeded the commonly accepted threshold of .70, indicating satisfactory
reliability for exploratory and design-based research purposes (Tabachnick & Fidell,
2001; Nunnally & Bernstein, 1994). Construct validity was further supported through
exploratory factor analysis, confirming the alignment between theoretical constructs and

Data Analysis
Quantitative   data were   analyzed   using descriptive statistics and        paired-sample
comparisons to examine changes in metacognitive regulation and speaking performance
across the intervention period. Effect sizes were calculated using Cohen's d for paired

samples and partial eta squared (η²) for multivariate analyses. Qualitative data from
learner perceptions and reflective responses were analyzed thematically following Braun
and Clarke's (2006) six-phase approach to identify recurring patterns related   to
metacognitive engagement and instructional design features. Integration of quantitative
and qualitative findings followed a convergent mixed-methods logic,       enabling
triangulation and richer interpretation of results (Creswell & Plano Clark, 2018).

To examine the effectiveness of the instructional design integrating microlearning and the
Feynman technique, the analytical procedure focused on testing inferences regarding the
relationship between metacognitive     regulation and academic     speaking         performance
following   the  intervention.     Prior to     hypothesis testing,    assumptions  of normality
(Shapiro-Wilk test), homogeneity of variance (Levene's test), and intercorrelation among
variables   were examined          and met     at acceptable levels    for multivariate analysis
(Tabachnick & Fidell, 2001).

RQ1: Effects on Metacognitive Regulation
To address Research Question 1 regarding the influence of the instructional intervention
on students' metacognitive regulation, a repeated-measures MANOVA was conducted
using the    three  metacognitive      dimensions (planning,     monitoring,     evaluation)  as
dependent variables. Results revealed a statistically significant multivariate effect of the
intervention on metacognitive regulation, Wilks' Λ = .41, F(3, 93) = 44.62, p < .001,
partial η² = .59. According to conventional benchmarks (Cohen, 1988), this value reflects

a large effect size, indicating that the instructional design accounted for a substantial
proportion of variance in students' metacognitive regulation.

Univariate follow-up analyses indicated differential effects across dimensions. Planning
showed a strong effect, F(1, 95) = 61.84, p < .001, partial η² = .39, followed by
evaluation, F(1, 95) = 68.12, p < .001, partial η² = .42. Monitoring also demonstrated a
statistically significant improvement, though with a comparatively moderate effect size,
F(1, 95) = 27.46, p < .001, partial η² = .22. These results suggest that the intervention was
particularly effective in fostering pre-task planning and post-task reflection (i.e., before
and after speaking), while in-task monitoring developed to a lesser yet still meaningful
extent. Table 5 presents the complete univariate ANOVA results.

Paired-sample comparisons further confirmed these findings. Planning demonstrated a

microlearning with the Feynman     technique significantly     enhances students'
metacognitive regulation in academic speaking, with the strongest effects observed in
planning and evaluation dimensions.

RQ2: Effects on Academic Speaking Performance
Research Question 2 examined how the instructional intervention affected students'
academic speaking performance across different dimensions. A paired-sample t-test
comparing pre- and post-intervention speaking scores revealed a statistically significant
increase in overall speaking performance, t(95) = 9.87, p < .001, with a large effect size
(Cohen's d = 1.01).

Further inspection of performance components revealed that gains were most pronounced
in idea organization and clarity of explanation. Idea organization demonstrated the largest
improvement, increasing from M = 2.76 (SD = 0.81) to M = 3.97 (SD = 0.68), MD =
1.21, Cohen's d = 1.15. Clarity of explanation also showed substantial gains, from M =
2.84 (SD = 0.79) to M = 3.92 (SD = 0.71), MD = 1.08, Cohen's d = 0.98. Strategic
delivery improved from M = 2.95 (SD = 0.76) to M = 3.79 (SD = 0.72), MD = 0.84,
Cohen's d    =          0.87. Responsiveness to audience cues showed the smallest but still
significant improvement, from M = 3.02 (SD = 0.74) to M = 3.81 (SD = 0.69), MD =
0.79, Cohen's d = 0.82.

RQ3: Student Perceptions of the Instructional Approach
Research Question    3 investigated students'    perceptions  regarding   the usefulness,
cognitive engagement, and instructional clarity of the integrated microlearning and
explain-to-learn approach. Both  quantitative    and    qualitative data were analyzed to
provide a comprehensive understanding of learner experiences.

Quantitative results from the perception survey indicated positive student responses
across all measured dimensions. Perceived usefulness received a mean rating of M = 4.23
(SD = 0.71) on a 5-point scale, with 78% of students rating this dimension at 4 or above.
Cognitive engagement showed similarly positive ratings (M = 4.15, SD = 0.76), with
74%    of students providing   ratings of  4 or  higher. Instructional   clarity was also
well-received (M = 4.08, SD = 0.79), suggesting that students found the structure and
sequencing of learning activities easy to follow.

Qualitative analysis of open-ended responses revealed three overarching themes. First,
students consistently reported that the segmented structure of microlearning modules
reduced cognitive overwhelm and enabled focused attention on specific speaking tasks.
Representative comments included: "The short videos helped me understand one thing at
a time without feeling overwhelmed" and "Breaking down the learning into small parts
made it easier to focus." This theme aligns with the CTML principle of segmentation and
supports the design decision to deliver content in manageable units.

Second, students valued the explain-to-learn activities as opportunities to consolidate
understanding and identify knowledge gaps. Comments in this theme included: "When I
had to explain the concept myself, I realized what I didn't really understand," "Recording
my explanation made me think more carefully about my words," and "Teaching the
concept   to myself  showed    me where    I was confused."   These responses     provide
qualitative evidence that the Feynman technique activated the generative processing
mechanisms posited by CTML, particularly the processes of organizing and integrating
information.

Third,  students   appreciated the metacognitive     prompts  as  tools   for  developing
self-awareness and self-regulation. Representative comments included: "The reflection

questions after each task made me think about how I can improve next time," "I started
planning my explanations more carefully because of the prompts," and "The questions
helped me notice when my explanation wasn't clear." These findings suggest that the
structured     reflection component     successfully scaffolded the development       of
metacognitive regulation, supporting the theoretical integration of CTML and SRL in the
instructional design.

Discussion
This study investigated how the integration of microlearning and the Feynman technique
fosters   metacognitive   regulation      as a mediating mechanism for academic speaking
development in higher education. Grounded in the Cognitive Theory of Multimedia
Learning and self-regulated learning theory, the research provides empirical evidence for
the pedagogical value of technology-mediated explain-to-learn approaches in language
education. The discussion addresses each research question in turn, situating findings
within the theoretical framework and prior research.

Interpretation of Findings on Metacognitive Regulation (RQ1)
The finding that the instructional intervention produced significant improvements in all
dimensions  of  metacognitive regulation     supports the theoretical prediction         that
explain-to-learn activities embedded in microlearning environments can systematically
activate regulatory processes. The large multivariate effect size (partial η² = .59) indicates
that the instructional design accounted for a substantial proportion of variance in students'
metacognitive  regulation, suggesting  that  the integration  of microlearning        and the
Feynman technique was effective in fostering the planning, monitoring, and evaluation
processes essential for self-regulated learning.

Notably, the strongest effects were observed in planning and evaluation, while monitoring
exhibited comparatively moderate gains. This asymmetry is theoretically meaningful and
aligns with prior research on metacognitive instruction. Planning and evaluation are
temporally distanced from the immediacy of speaking performance and thus more
amenable to instructional scaffolding through prompts, checklists, and reflective tasks.
Monitoring, by contrast, unfolds in real time under cognitive load, resembling what might
be described  as thinking  on one's            feet—regulating cognition while simultaneously
producing language. The relative resistance of monitoring to instructional change has
been  reported  in previous   studies  and      underscores the  challenge of       fostering
in-the-moment metacognitive control through short-term interventions (Zhang et al.,
2021; Sabnani & Goh, 2022; Teng & Zhang, 2016).

From a CTML perspective, the differential effects across metacognitive dimensions can
be understood through the lens of cognitive load management. Planning and evaluation
occur outside the primary speaking task, allowing learners to devote cognitive resources
to              regulatory processes without the simultaneous demands of language production.
Monitoring, however, must compete with the cognitive resources required for real-time
speaking, making it more difficult to develop through instructional scaffolding alone.
Future designs might incorporate additional supports for in-task monitoring, such as
real-time feedback or structured pauses during speaking tasks.

Interpretation of Findings on Academic Speaking Performance (RQ2)
The significant improvement in academic speaking performance (Cohen's d = 1.01)
provides evidence that the instructional intervention successfully enhanced students'
ability to communicate academic content orally. The pattern of gains—most pronounced
in idea organization and clarity of explanation—aligns closely with the explain-to-learn
logic underpinning the     Feynman    technique.    By requiring  learners to  externalize
understanding through explanation, the intervention activated the generative processing
mechanisms      posited by       CTML, particularly the processes of  selecting  essential
information, organizing ideas coherently, and integrating new understanding with prior
knowledge.

The relatively smaller gains in responsiveness to audience cues may reflect the nature of
the intervention, which focused primarily on individual explanation tasks rather than
interactive  communication.   While   the Feynman  technique     emphasizes    clarity and
coherence    in explanation,  it does not inherently require     learners  to  adapt their
communication in response to real-time audience feedback. Future iterations of the
instructional design might incorporate more interactive elements to foster this dimension
of speaking competence.

The           strong correlations observed between metacognitive regulation dimensions and
speaking performance (ranging from r = .54 to r = .71) support the theoretical proposition
that metacognitive regulation functions as a mediating mechanism linking instructional
design to learning outcomes. Students who demonstrated stronger regulatory capabilities
also  showed    greater       improvements in speaking  performance, suggesting that   the
development of metacognitive skills was not merely an incidental outcome but a critical
pathway through which the intervention produced its effects.

Interpretation of Findings on Student Perceptions (RQ3)
The positive student perceptions reported across all dimensions—perceived usefulness,
cognitive engagement, and instructional clarity—provide important qualitative support
for the quantitative findings on learning outcomes. The high ratings for perceived
usefulness (M = 4.23) suggest that students recognized the value of the integrated

approach   for their   learning, which  may have contributed     to their motivation and
engagement throughout the intervention.

The three themes emerging from qualitative analysis—segmentation reducing cognitive
overwhelm, explain-to-learn consolidating understanding, and metacognitive prompts
developing      self-awareness—provide  rich evidence    that students  experienced  the
instructional design as intended. The alignment between student reports and theoretical
predictions strengthens confidence in the interpretation that the intervention operated
through  the   hypothesized mechanisms     of generative processing    and metacognitive
regulation.

Comparison with Prior Research
The effect sizes observed in this study (partial η² = .59 for metacognitive regulation,
Cohen's d = 1.01 for speaking performance) are comparable to or exceed those reported
in previous    studies examining metacognitive   instruction  in  language learning. For
example,   Teng and    Zhang    (2016)  reported Cohen's d    =   0.72 for the effect of
metacognitive instruction on writing performance, while Zhang et al. (2021) found
moderate effects (r = .45) for the relationship between metacognitive awareness and
speaking performance. The comparatively stronger effects in this study may be attributed
to the systematic integration of metacognitive scaffolding within a technology-mediated
environment, as opposed to more generic metacognitive instruction.

The findings also align with research on generative learning strategies, which consistently
demonstrates that activities requiring learners to actively explain concepts produce deeper
learning than   passive study    (Fiorella & Mayer, 2016;     Chi   & Wylie, 2014). The
integration of the Feynman technique within microlearning modules represents a novel
application of these principles to academic speaking instruction, extending prior research
on explain-to-learn strategies to a new domain and modality.


Limitations
Several limitations of this study should be acknowledged. First, the study relied on a
single institutional context and a relatively modest sample size (N = 96), limiting the
generalizability of the findings to diverse higher education settings. Replication studies

across  different institutional contexts, cultural backgrounds, and        language learning
environments would strengthen the evidence base for the instructional approach.

Second, the use of self-report measures for metacognitive regulation, while common in
the literature, may not fully capture learners' real-time regulatory behavior. Self-report
instruments assess learners' perceptions of their regulatory processes rather than their
actual cognitive activities during task performance. Future research could incorporate
process-oriented data sources such as stimulated recall interviews, think-aloud protocols,
or learning analytics (e.g., pause patterns, reformulations during speech) to triangulate
self-reported regulation with behavioral measures (Winne & Jamieson-Noel, 2002).

Third, the eight-week intervention period, while sufficient to observe meaningful changes
in metacognitive regulation and speaking performance, may not capture the full trajectory
of           skill development. Longitudinal studies examining sustained effects over longer
periods (e.g., semester-long or year-long interventions) would provide valuable insights
into the durability of learning gains and the potential for continued development with
extended practice.

Fourth,  the design-based nature of     the study  prioritizes explanatory insight       and
instructional refinement over broad causal generalization. While the iterative approach
strengthens internal validity and produces actionable design principles, the findings
should be interpreted as evidence of what is possible under specific conditions rather than
universal claims about the effectiveness of the intervention across all contexts.

Fifth, the study did not include a control group receiving alternative instruction, limiting
the ability to make strong causal claims about the unique contributions of the integrated
approach    compared to   other instructional methods. Future   research           employing
randomized controlled designs would strengthen causal inferences and provide more
definitive  evidence of      the intervention's efficacy relative to baseline or alternative
conditions.

Recommendations for Future Research
Based on the findings and limitations of this study, several directions for future research
are recommended:

First, future studies should investigate the long-term sustainability of metacognitive
regulation and speaking performance gains through longitudinal designs. Understanding
whether the benefits observed in this eight-week intervention persist over time—and
under what conditions—is critical for assessing the practical value of the instructional
approach.

Second,  research should explore the     integration of    process-oriented measures     to
complement self-report  assessments  of    metacognitive   regulation. Methods  such     as
stimulated recall, discourse analysis of spoken output, and learning analytics could
provide richer, more nuanced understanding of how regulatory processes unfold during
academic speaking tasks.

Third,  comparative studies  examining     the integrated  microlearning and        Feynman
technique approach against alternative instructional methods would clarify the unique
contributions of each component. Such research could address questions about whether
the benefits  observed  are  attributable  to  microlearning structure,  explain-to-learn
activities, their integration, or some combination thereof.

Fourth, research should investigate the applicability of the instructional approach across
diverse learner populations, including different proficiency levels, academic disciplines,
and cultural contexts. Understanding how       the intervention     functions across varied
populations would inform adaptations needed for broader implementation.

Fifth, future designs might explore the incorporation of additional supports for in-task
monitoring, which showed comparatively smaller gains in this study. Strategies such as
real-time feedback, structured pauses during speaking, or peer interaction components
could potentially enhance the development of real-time regulatory capabilities.

Practical Implications
The findings of this study carry several practical implications for educators, instructional
designers, and policymakers in higher education language instruction:

For educators, the study demonstrates that metacognitive regulation can be systematically
fostered through carefully designed instructional activities. Rather than assuming that
metacognitive          skills develop incidentally through language practice, instructors can
explicitly scaffold planning, monitoring, and evaluation processes through structured
prompts and reflective tasks. The specific sequence of concept exposure, explain-to-learn
activity, and metacognitive reflection provides a replicable model for designing speaking
instruction that targets regulatory processes.

For instructional     designers,  the study  offers evidence-based      design principles for
technology-enhanced    language   learning   environments. Key        principles include: (1)
segmenting content into manageable units to reduce cognitive overload, (2) embedding
generative   learning  activities that  require active   explanation  rather than     passive
consumption,  (3)      providing  structured metacognitive       prompts at key points in the
learning process, and (4) integrating audio recording and reflection features that enable
self-assessment and iterative improvement.

For policymakers, the findings support contemporary orientations emphasizing deep
learning and communication competence in higher education. The Kampus Berdampak
and Pembelajaran Mendalam frameworks in Indonesia, as well as similar initiatives
globally, call for instructional practices that move beyond surface-level skill development
toward meaningful knowledge construction and metacognitive reflection. This study
provides    empirical  evidence   that    technology-mediated instructional  designs      can
effectively support these policy goals when grounded in established learning theories and
implemented with attention to cognitive and metacognitive processes.

For technology developers, the study highlights the importance of designing learning
platforms that support generative processing and metacognitive regulation rather than
merely    delivering   content.  Features such as   audio recording,  structured   reflection
prompts, and progress tracking can transform microlearning environments from passive

content delivery systems into active learning tools that foster deeper engagement and
self-regulated learning.


Conclusion
This study contributes to the growing body of research that conceptualizes learning as a
dynamic, regulated          process shaped by instructional design and learner agency. By
demonstrating how microlearning integrated with the Feynman technique can foster
metacognitive regulation as a mediating mechanism for academic speaking development,
the study     advances   a    process-oriented   perspective    that moves         beyond
achievement-focused paradigms.

The findings  provide   robust empirical  support  for the   theoretical proposition that
metacognitive regulation functions    as  a  critical pathway   linking  explain-to-learn
instructional design to speaking performance outcomes. The large effect sizes observed
for both metacognitive regulation (partial η² = .59) and speaking performance (Cohen's d
= 1.01) demonstrate that the integrated approach produced meaningful, educationally
significant improvements in student learning.

The positive student perceptions reported across all dimensions—perceived usefulness,
cognitive engagement, and instructional clarity—suggest that the intervention was not
only effective but also well-received by learners. The alignment between quantitative
outcomes and qualitative experiences strengthens confidence in the interpretation that the
intervention operated through the hypothesized mechanisms of generative processing and
metacognitive regulation.

Such a perspective invites further inquiry into how higher education language instruction
can cultivate sustainable communicative competence, positioning academic speaking not
merely as an assessed outcome but as a reflective practice that evolves through guided
regulation and purposeful engagement. The design principles and theoretical insights
generated through this   research provide   a foundation for    continued   innovation in
technology-enhanced   language education,    with  implications extending beyond      the
specific context of academic speaking to broader questions about how digital learning
environments can support self-regulated learning across domains.
